## Core stance snapshot

- **Inflation**: Absolute priority, zero tolerance for persistence, anti-"transitory" narrative
- **Growth**: Price stability >> employment stability; opposes growth-inflation tradeoff
- **Financial stability**: Crisis intervention支持, but requires strict ex-ante regulation to minimize crisis frequency
- **Independence**: Conditional - earned through narrow focus + good results, not an entitlement; applies only to monetary policy, not regulation
- **Balance sheet**: Crisis tool only; opposes QE in normal times; demands symmetric treatment (if growth=MP, then runoff=MP)
- **Communication strategy**: Minimize (not maximize); "page B12" in normal times; avoid forward guidance除非crisis

## Triggers and thresholds

### Inflation triggers
- **Level**:
  - 2-4%: Concern, monitor closely
  - 4-5%: Action threshold - should begin tightening
  - >5% sustained: Immediate aggressive response required
  - >9%: Credibility crisis ("greatest mistake in 45 years")

- **Persistence**: Immediate concern if >2 consecutive readings above target
- **Expectations**: Critical - any sign of de-anchoring → immediate tightening
- **Tolerance**: Near-zero; rejects "average inflation targeting" or "make-up" strategies

**Key quotes**:
- [38:50, G30-2025]: "Inflation was 2% because they said so" - critiques self-executing assumption
- [2021-06, WSJ]: When CPI ~5%, Warsh called for immediate tightening
- [2025, G30]: 9.1% peak = "greatest mistake in macroeconomic policy in 45 years"

**Historical calibration** (2021-2022):
- Mid-2021: CPI reached 5%+ → Warsh warned immediately
- Dec 2021: CPI 7%+ → "Fed is main culprit"
- June 2022: CPI 9.1% → "Greatest mistake in 45 years"
- **Implied threshold**: >4-5% sustained requires immediate action

**Reaction**: Tighten immediately, communicate commitment to price stability, reject "transitory" narratives

### Growth triggers
- **Output gap**: Skeptical of real-time measurement; focuses on inflation signals instead
- **Labor market stress**: Rejects "inclusive employment" redefinition
  - Quote [35:30]: "The Fed redefined maximum employment as a broad-based and inclusive goal"
  - Position: Should focus on individuals, not groups

**Principle** [36:45]:
> "What an individual does when he or she wakes up in the morning matters immeasurably to the path of the economy."

**Reaction**: Will not ease for employment if inflation elevated; price stability first

---

### Financial stability triggers

**Panic vs. Recession Framework** (2009 FRASER):

**Panic definition**:
> [2009-04, FRASER] "Loss of confidence in the financial system, when even sound firms find it difficult to borrow. Breakdown in trust where once firmly held truths are no longer relied upon."

**Quantitative panic indicators** (2009):
- Household wealth decline: >-15 to -20%
- GDP contraction: <-6% in single quarter
- Stock market: >-40% annual decline
- Bank market cap: >-50% decline
- Unemployment: Rapid surge >8-9%
- Money market dysfunction: Freezes, runs, spread blowouts

**Crisis Threshold**:
```
IF 2+ panic indicators triggered
THEN systemic crisis requiring extraordinary intervention
```

**Examples**:
- 2008: Supported aggressive intervention
  - Quote [2009-04, FRASER]: "Necessary interventions" (QE1)
  - Quote [27:45, G30-2025]: "We strongly supported this crisis time innovation"
- 2023 SVB: Critical of response
  - Quote [54:10, G30-2025]: "We didn't do as good a job as we could have"

**Reaction**:
- **Panic/Crisis**: Aggressive balance sheet expansion, lender of last resort
  - Quote [2010-03, FRASER]: "Fed must stay foursquare within its role as liquidity provider"
- **Post-crisis**: "Retrace steps" immediately when panic subsides
  - Quote [2009-04, FRASER]: "The Panic began before the recession and will assuredly end before it"
- **Normal times**: Strict supervision to prevent next crisis, ruthless pragmatism evaluating gaps

---

### Fiscal risk triggers

**Red lines**:
- Fed buying government debt in normal times
- Fiscal authority expecting Fed accommodation
- Spending >> revenue growth with Fed enabling

**Current assessment** [25:40, G30-2025]:
> "US federal government is spending in excess of 60% more than the day before COVID"

**Trigger for concern**: When QE occurs outside crisis context
- Quote [28:10]: "I worried mightily in fall of 2010, a time of strong growth and financial stability, the decision to buy still more Treasury bonds would involve the Fed in the messy political business of fiscal policy"

**Reaction**: Public call for fiscal discipline (if crossing into fiscal domain); otherwise, maintain separation

## Policy tools preference

### Rates
- **Preference**: Primary tool, always
- **Philosophy**: Simple, transparent, reversible
- **Criticism of alternatives**: Data dependence is "false precision" [20:45]

**Quote** [20:30]:
> "There's only two problems with data dependence, the data and the dependence"

### Balance sheet
- **Crisis times**: Aggressive use justified (2009 position)
  1. Monetary policy accommodation when at ZLB
  2. Market liquidity provision when markets dysfunctional
  - Quote [2009-04, FRASER]: Supported QE1 as "necessary interventions"

- **Normal times**: Strong opposition (2010 position → resignation)
  - Quote [2010-06, FRASER]: "Past the point of diminishing returns"
  - Quote [2010-11, FRASER]: "Expanding Fed's balance sheet is not a free option"
  - **Risks**: Currency weakness, commodity inflation, price stability compromise

- **Treasury market threshold** [2010-11, FRASER]:
  > "Fed risked becoming more of a price maker than a price taker in Treasury markets"
  - **Red line**: Fed purchasing >30-40% of new issuance
  - **Consequence**: Obscures fiscal sustainability signals

- **Post-crisis principle** [2009-04, FRASER]:
  > "When panics subside, Fed should retrace its steps"
  - Must normalize quickly, not maintain crisis footing

**Symmetry demand** [01:00:00-01:00:30, G30-2025 Q&A]:
> "If it was monetary policy when we grew the balance sheet, how could it not be monetary policy on the other end?"

**Target**: Return to ~$1 trillion or less (pre-2008 scale)

**Historical action**: Resigned Feb 2011 over QE2 continuation in normal times

### Forward guidance
- **Normal times**: Avoid; "unhelpful to deliberations" [22:45]
- **Crisis times**: Acceptable as commitment device

**Quote** [22:50]:
> "Central bank should find new comfort in working without applause and without the audience at the edge of its seats"

**Philosophy**: Preserve optionality, avoid becoming "prisoners of their own words" [21:40]

### Regulatory posture
- **Philosophy**: Strict ex-ante supervision to minimize crisis frequency
- **Accountability**: Ruthless pragmatism about failures (e.g., SVB 2023)
- **Independence**: Does NOT apply to bank regulation

**Quote** [41:35]:
> "I do not believe the Fed is owed any particular deference in bank regulatory or supervisory policy"

## Priority ordering

### 1. Price Stability
**Absolute first priority**

**Evidence**:
- Resigned over QE2 in 2010 (growth strong, but opposed easing)
- Calls inflation failure loss of "plot armor" [40:15]
- Rejects any growth-inflation tradeoff

**Threshold**: Will tighten even if growth slowing, if inflation above target

---

### 2. Financial Stability
**Second priority, but with caveats**

**Crisis mode**: Will cross traditional boundaries
- Supported 2008 interventions
- Accepts Volcker critique that Fed "went to very edge of lawful power" [16:40]

**Normal mode**: Focus on prevention via regulation
- Quote [24:50]: "When panics subside, Fed is duty bound to retrace its steps"

**Accountability**: Must evaluate regulatory failures honestly (SVB 2023)

---

### 3. Central Bank Independence
**Instrumental, not intrinsic value**

**Definition** [2010-03, FRASER]:
> "The Fed is not independent from government. It is independent within government."
- Congress can modify Fed's charter
- Independence applies to **monetary policy conduct only**
- Does NOT extend to regulation, consumer protection

**Foundation** [2010-03, FRASER]:
> "Institutional credibility is the real money multiplier in the conduct of policy"
- Greatest asset = credibility
- Earned through consistent, considered judgments

**Quote** [41:00, G30-2025]:
> "Independence is not a policy goal unto itself. It's a means of achieving certain important policy outcomes"

**Conditions to Earn Independence**:
1. Narrow focus on congressionally-mandated remit
2. Competent execution (especially price stability)
3. Avoid mission creep (climate, DEI, etc.)
4. Accept accountability when fail
5. Stay within liquidity provider role (not capital allocator)

**Threats to Independence** [2010-03, FRASER]:
- External: Governments encouraging loose policy to finance debt
- Internal: Accepting "a little more inflation for less unemployment"
- Mission creep: Becoming "repair shop for broken statutes"

**Quote** [43:05, G30-2025]:
> "I believe Fed independence is chiefly up to the Fed"

**Consistency**: 2010 → 2025 完全一致

---

### 4. Employment / Growth
**Not neglected, but strictly subordinate to price stability**

**Rejects**:
- "Inclusive employment" redefinition
- Group-based policy targeting
- Employment-first easing when inflation elevated

**Supports**:
- Individual economic opportunity
- Supply-side policies (not Fed's job, but economy-wide priority)

## Conditional statements (quote-backed)

### On Inflation:

**IF** inflation persistently above target
**THEN** tighten immediately without waiting for employment weakness
**BECAUSE** "The steepest price of the new policy regime has been paid for the last five years by the supposed beneficiaries" [36:25, G30-2025]

**IF** inflation expectations show signs of de-anchoring
**THEN** front-load tightening to restore credibility
**BECAUSE** "Central bank credibility is the coin that purchases American economic strength" [20:10, G30-2025]

---

### On Balance Sheet:

**IF** in crisis with rates at zero lower bound
**THEN** expand balance sheet aggressively
**BECAUSE** "We cut interest rates to zero and it wasn't enough" [27:35, G30-2025]

**IF** in normal times with full employment and growth
**THEN** runoff balance sheet to pre-crisis levels
**BECAUSE** "QE2 was announced. I disagreed with the path of policy and resigned" [28:15, G30-2025]

**IF** balance sheet growth is called "monetary policy"
**THEN** balance sheet runoff must also be treated as monetary policy
**BECAUSE** "If it was monetary policy when we grew the balance sheet, how could it not be monetary policy on the other end?" [01:00:05, G30 Q&A]

---

### On Fiscal Policy:

**IF** Fed buys government debt in normal times
**THEN** risks "monetary dominance" over fiscal policy
**BECAUSE** "The line between the central bank and the ostensible fiscal authority has grown harder to identify" [30:10, G30-2025]

**IF** Fed comments on fiscal policy in crisis
**THEN** must also advocate discipline in good times (symmetry)
**BECAUSE** "If Fed chooses to cross that line under exigent circumstances, there should be real and rhetorical symmetry" [26:35, G30-2025]

**IF** government spending growth far exceeds plausible revenue growth
**THEN** Fed should not enable via QE
**BECAUSE** "60% more spending than before COVID. No plausible economic growth can deliver revenues to match that surge" [25:55, G30-2025]

---

### On Independence:

**IF** Fed stays narrowly focused on price stability + full employment
**THEN** deserves operational independence in monetary policy
**BECAUSE** "Our constitutional republic is accepting of an independent central bank only if it sticks to its congressionally directed duty and successfully performs its task" [43:32, G30-2025]

**IF** Fed wanders into non-core areas (climate, social issues)
**THEN** jeopardizes case for independence
**BECAUSE** "The more the Fed opines on matters outside of its remit, the more it jeopardizes its ability to ensure stable prices and full employment" [37:53, G30-2025]

**IF** Fed fails on core mandate (price stability)
**THEN** should accept strong oversight and accountability
**BECAUSE** "When monetary policy outcomes are poor, the Fed should be subjected to serious questioning, strong oversight, and even opprobrium" [43:15, G30-2025]

---

### On Financial Stability:

**IF** banking system faces solvency threat
**THEN** aggressive intervention justified
**BECAUSE** "In my judgment had the Fed not stood in, the global banking system would have proven insolvent" [52:40, G30 Q&A]

**IF** post-crisis regulations fail (e.g., SVB 2023)
**THEN** must ruthlessly evaluate what went wrong
**BECAUSE** "We central bankers said after '08, 'Never again.' Well, the natural experiment revealed some weaknesses" [53:30, G30 Q&A]

**IF** between crises in normal times
**THEN** Fed should retrace steps and normalize
**BECAUSE** "The more we comport in our daily business like every day is a crisis, the more we ask for greater crises" [55:17, G30 Q&A]

---

### On Tools & Communication:

**IF** making near-term forecasts public
**THEN** policymakers "become prisoners of their own words" [21:40, G30-2025]
**RECOMMENDATION**: Skip opportunities to share latest musings

**IF** using forward guidance in normal times
**THEN** "tempting but unhelpful" [22:45, G30-2025]
**RECOMMENDATION**: Reserve for crisis only

**IF** central bank is data-dependent
**THEN** risks "false precision" and "analytic complacency" [21:00, G30-2025]
**RECOMMENDATION**: Focus on best decision, not perfect forecast
